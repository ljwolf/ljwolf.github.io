Where are the rentals?

The map and table below show a bit about the rental market based on an analysis of listings in a large property listing website in Bristol. Here, we show the total number of listings in each ward by year, as well as the percent of listings that we can identify as targeted for students. Select the layer you want to visualize using the blue checkboxes to the right of the map. Also, the information on each ward is present if you hover over it with your mouse (or tap if you’re on a phone!).

You can use the pair of “# Listings” and “% Student” layers in tandem, too. If you tick the checkbox for the “# Listings” layer in 2019, and then turn on the “% Student” layer for 2019, then the two colors will blend. In this case, purple areas are areas where ther eare a lot of listings and lots of listings that students can rent. In contrast, areas that become blue are areas with fewer listings, but where most of those listings are student listings. Wards that remain red are areas with many rental listings, but few student-friendly listings in that ward.

What price brackets in the rental market are changing the quickest?

This section provides information on what price brackets of the market are changing the quickest. Here, we refer to a few different types of non-student rental properties. The Bottom segment refers to properies that have rents in the lowest 10% advertised for that kind of property in that ward. So, if the Bottom rent is £80 per week, then 10% of properties are advertising rent of £80 per week or less. Likewise, the Top of the market segment refers to properties that are in the highest 10% advertised. The Lower segment refers to properties that have rents advertised in the lowest 25%, and likewise for the Upper segment at the more expensive end of the rental ladder. The “Middle” segment refers to the median advertised rent in that year and that ward.

Thus, the following panels describe the change in these market segments over time. This lets you tell who rent is changing for, and where. For example, the cheapest of 1 bedrooms in Horfield have actually been getting cheaper, because the lowest 10% of advertised rent decreased over 2018 to 2020. But, the median rent in Horfield shot up by nearly 18% over the same period. Thus, you can think of this as showing the rate of change for properties in a price bracket.

You can also see that, in terms of the price per bedroom (which standardizes out the different property types), rentals are generlaly getting more expensive overall at about 11 to 12% from 2018 to 2020.

The color on the maps displays the price change in the median of the market, but hovering over each ward will display the change in each price bracket. Also, the table below the map displays the same information.
