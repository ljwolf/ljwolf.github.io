Geodetector

In general, I think the Q-statistic is a very helpful diagnostic for understanding the variance partitioning across a spatial domain. But,

Our axiom is that if \(X\) causes \(Y\), then their spatial patterns (spatial stratified heterogeneities) would tend to be coupled, in addition to displaying a significant Pearson’s correlation.

This “axiom” from the new Wang et al. (2024) paper published in the Annals is only true if \(X\) is a sole cause of \(y\).

The simplest case where this can be invalidated is, for example, a spatially-patterned omitted mediator variable:

import numpy
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn
numpy.random.seed(112112)
X = numpy.arange(100) + numpy.random.normal(scale=5, size=100)
M = numpy.arange(100)[::-1] + numpy.random.normal(scale=5, size=100)
y = X*M + numpy.random.normal(size=100)

In this case, \(X\) causes \(y\) through a mediator variable. However, the mediator “filters” the pattern of \(X\), resulting in an outcome that is quite distinct from the pattern of \(X\):

Xm = X.reshape(10,10)
Mm = M.reshape(10,10)
ym = y.reshape(10,10)

f,ax = plt.subplots(1,3,figsize=(8,4))
ax[0].imshow(Xm)
ax[1].imshow(Mm)
ax[2].imshow(ym)
ax[0].set_title("X")
ax[1].set_title("Mediator (omitted)")
ax[2].set_title("Outcome")
plt.show()

You can see this case that the mediator is the inverse pattern of \(X\), the causal effect we observe. Due to the relationship caused by the causal diagram above, this is akin to a generating function like \(y=-x^2 + x\) where the maximum is aligned with the horizontal axis, towards the middle of the vertical axis. Regardless, this results in zero correlation because of nonlinear structure in the relationship:

seaborn.regplot(x=X, y=y)
plt.title(f"Correlation = {numpy.corrcoef(X,y)[0,1]:.2f}")
plt.show()

I think it’s pretty important to latch onto the concepts of causality from wider social sciences and computer science literature. Indeed, I’ve just gotten back from a Dagstuhl seminar on spatial causal inference!

But, this paper, despite its interesting new statistic, does not clearly ground this notion of causality in a specific process, or even a representation of a process that is amenable to classical concepts like spatially-patterned mediators, confounders, spillover, or interference. And, as a consequence, this section is pretty off-base from the rest of the paper, and leaves it pretty vulnerable to a simple counterexample like above!
